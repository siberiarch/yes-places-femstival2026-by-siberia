01 — The Problem

"When was the last time a space welcomed you without asking for anything in return?"

As Women, we understand what our existence means in the eyes of the market: to consume or to be consumed. That same logic is reflected in the built environment and in the way our cities are planned.

The shopping mall is perhaps its most honest expression: a space designed for flow, transactions, and brief stays. A place that welcomes our spending, but not our presence. Designed without our input and optimized without our needs in mind.

Unfortunately, this trend is only becoming more widespread. More and more spaces are being built to maximize profit and accelerate the flow of capital rather than support the well-being of communities. It is no coincidence that, at the same time, third places, those informal, accessible gathering spaces that sustain social life and community, are disappearing.

The demand for genuine, physical connection has never been greater, yet there are increasingly fewer places where that need can be fulfilled.

Women shoulder a disproportionate share of domestic labor

In Latin America, women spend nearly triple the time men do on unpaid care work, and 47% of women outside the labor force cite care responsibilities as their main barrier.

Source: ILO / CEPAL, 2024

Concerns about safety make many public spaces less accessible

9 in 10 women aged 18–29 in Lima have experienced sexual harassment in public space. In Bogotá and Mexico City, 6 in 10 women experience harassment on public transport.

Source: Latinoamérica 21, 2026

Fewer third places exist within walking distance

When third places disappear, people interact less, feel more isolated, and lose their sense of belonging.

Source: CU Boulder, 2025

The shopping mall becomes the most accessible option, but access is conditioned by consumption

The mall replaced the plaza. It looks like public space, but functions as private space.

Source: Sustainability (MDPI), 2021

Opportunities for social connection and community building are diminished

Feeling welcome in public space is associated with a 21% decrease in loneliness.

Source: King's College London / World Cities Culture Forum, 2025
02 — Objectives
What we should aim for
Reclaiming the space

Between Augé's non-place and Oldenburg's third space, there is an unoccupied territory. A space that does not just allow encounter — it actively produces it. A space that cares. A space with permanence. A space that belongs to its community.

Yes-Place is a spatial category defined by three simultaneous criteria. Without all three, it is just a commercial space with alternative aesthetics.

01
Local authorship

Art and culture are produced in place itself — not imported, not externally validated. The creator is from here. The work speaks from here.

02
Community economy

The economic benefits generated by cultural activation remain within the community. Value is produced for the people who built it, not extracted by external actors.

03
Permanence without consumption

The space receives you even if you don't buy anything. You can stay. You can exist without justification. Being here is enough.

03 — Evidence

Real life cases that sustain our hypothesis

The concept of "Yes-Places" is not exactly new or groundbreaking. Actually, they already exist in practice. This initiative comes from the need of naming the act of reclaiming the space and transforming it back into a third space.

Bogotá, Colombia
District Care System

Ordinary urban infrastructure converted into territorial care hubs for women and caregivers. The care logic that public policy applies at city scale — Yes-Place brings it to a local commercial space.

Berlin Impact Award 2023
Istanbul, Turkey
Karaköy: From Commerce to Art

Historically commercial streets transformed through collective artistic interventions. Documented academically in 2024 as a direct case of commercial-to-community spatial conversion.

Direct empirical case
Paris, France
Montparnasse Tower

Renzo Piano commissioned in 2022 to transform a 1970s shopping center into an open, people-centered pedestrian district. Even institutional architecture recognizes the commercial typology can be something else.

Institutional scale
Mexico City, Mexico
UTOPÍAS Reframed

The UTOPÍAS program — which converts public spaces into community centers — was explicitly reframed through a feminist and care-centered lens in 2023. Private commercial spaces are the logical next frontier.

Gap Yes-Place fills
